Innovation:
Automated Medical Research Information Exchange System
This patent introduces a system, method, and computer-readable medium for streamlining the exchange of medical research information between healthcare providers and research sponsors (e.g., pharmaceutical companies, medical device manufacturers). The innovation addresses the inefficiencies, errors, and delays in traditional clinical trial data collection and review processes by:

Automating the extraction, validation, and transformation of subject information (e.g., patient records, lab results, diagnostic images) from Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) or direct healthcare sources.
Verifying compliance of subject data with research protocols and schedules (e.g., ensuring lab tests align with trial timelines).
Transforming data from source formats (e.g., HL7) into standardized formats (e.g., CDISC) required by regulatory agencies (e.g., FDA, EMA).
Flagging discrepancies (e.g., missing data, protocol deviations) and resolving errors in real-time, reducing manual intervention.
Enabling secure, auditable data flows with status flags and audit trails to ensure transparency and compliance (e.g., HIPAA, 21 CFR Part 11).
Integrating with research systems (e.g., Clinical Trial Management Systems (CTMS), Clinical Data Management Systems (CDMS)) to accelerate regulatory submissions and drug/device approvals.

Abstract:
The patent describes a medical research information exchange system that automates the collection, validation, transformation, and delivery of clinical trial data from healthcare providers to research sponsors. The system:

Receives research project information (e.g., protocols, schedules) and subject information (e.g., patient records, lab results) from sources like HIEs, EHRs, or PACS.
Verifies subject information against protocol requirements, flagging non-compliant data (e.g., missing tests, incorrect timelines).
Transforms data from source formats (e.g., HL7) to standardized formats (e.g., CDISC ODM/SDTM) using mapping dictionaries (e.g., BRIDG).
Anonymizes data to protect patient privacy (e.g., removing PHI per HIPAA).
Provides standardized data to research systems (e.g., CTMS, CDMS) for regulatory submissions (e.g., FDA, EMA).
Generates audit trails with status flags to track data processing and ensure compliance.
The system reduces manual errors, speeds up data processing, and lowers costs by eliminating redundant data entry and on-site visits, ultimately accelerating clinical trials and drug/device approvals.

Methodology:
1. Data Collection:

Sources:

Health Information Exchanges (HIEs): Aggregate patient data from hospitals, labs, pharmacies, and EHRs.
Direct Healthcare Organizations: EHR/EMR systems, radiology (PACS/RIS), Medicare/Medicaid (CMS), pharmacies, and insurance providers.
Subject Consent: Ensures Informed Consent Agreements (ICAs) are signed before data extraction.

Formats:

Source Formats: HL7, proprietary EHR formats, CCR/CCD/CDA.
Standardized Formats: CDISC (ODM, SDTM), CTMS/CDMS-compatible formats.

2. Data Verification:

Protocol Compliance:

Checks if subject data aligns with trial protocols (e.g., inclusion/exclusion criteria, visit schedules).
Example: Verifies a diabetic patient’s HbA1c test was performed at the correct visit (e.g., "Visit 2").

Schedule Adherence:

Maps subject data to trial timelines (e.g., "Day 1: Baseline ECG," "Day 30: Follow-up Lab").
Flags missed appointments, late tests, or out-of-range results (e.g., blood pressure >180/120).

Discrepancy Handling:

Automated flags for errors (e.g., duplicate records, missing fields).
Manual review options for Clinical Research Associates (CRAs) to annotate/resolve issues.

3. Data Transformation:

Format Conversion:

Uses mapping dictionaries (e.g., BRIDG Release 3.2) to convert HL7 → CDISC ODM/SDTM.
Example: Transforms a lab result in HL7 to a standardized CDISC SDTM domain (e.g., "LB" for lab data).

Anonymization:

Removes Personally Identifiable Information (PII/PHI) per HIPAA/GDPR.
Assigns pseudonymous IDs to subjects for privacy.

Model Generation:

Custom models for unique trials (e.g., oncology vs. cardiovascular).
Reusable templates for common data types (e.g., vitals, adverse events).

4. Data Delivery:

Research Systems Integration:

CTMS: For trial management (e.g., patient enrollment, site monitoring).
CDMS: For data cleaning, coding (e.g., MedDRA for adverse events), and database locking.
Regulatory Databases: FDA’s Sentinel Initiative, EMA’s EudraVigilance.

Access Control:

Role-based permissions (e.g., CRAs view raw data; DMs access cleaned data).
Audit Trails: Logs all actions (e.g., "Data transformed on 2023-10-15 by User X").

5. Automation and Scalability:

Scheduled Extractions:

Automated requests to HIEs (e.g., daily pulls for new lab results).
Event triggers (e.g., "Extract data when Visit 3 is completed").

Real-Time Processing:

Near real-time validation/transformation (e.g., data available to sponsors within hours).
APIs for seamless integration with EHRs/CTMS.

Scalable Architecture:

Cloud-based or on-premise deployment.
Supports multi-site, global trials (e.g., FDA + EMA compliance).

6. Compliance and Security:

Regulatory Standards:

21 CFR Part 11 (electronic records/signatures).
GCP/ICH Guidelines (Good Clinical Practice).

Data Security:

Encryption (e.g., TLS for data in transit, AES-256 for data at rest).
Access Logs for audits.
